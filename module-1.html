The Biology

You didn't evolve for this.

You might've heard before that your brain is anatomically ancient. But how ancient are we talking, and why would that even matter for us today?

Through analyzing patterns in the size and shape of our ancestors' fossilized skulls, anthropologists currently estimate that the human brain became anatomically modern somewhere between 35,000 and 100,000 years ago. To put the more conservative of those estimates in perspective: we're talking about somewhere around the time your great 1,200x great-grandparents were alive.

Even though our modern societies often protect us from many of the deadly threats our ancestors used to face every day, the reality is that your brain and body are still fundamentally built to ensure your survival in a dangerous and unpredictable environment.

You come naturally equipped with some fascinating, highly responsive biological systems designed to keep you alive. A variety of subdisciplines within the biological sciences (such as neuroscience and endocrinology) have done a great job helping us understand what these systems are and how they work.

But researchers in labs aren't the only ones paying attention: companies who build the technology we use every day are translating findings from the same science to actively run behavioral experiments on us as their users, and get the most out of our engagement. Now they know exactly what buttons to push in you, so you keep pushing buttons for them.

So, while you might know you're just looking at a video on your phone's screen, your body doesn't actually know that. And while it might not seem like a big deal in the moment, the compounding effects of this on your health significantly add up over time.

Dopamine · The Anticipation Chemical
🎰

The More Uncertain You Feel, The More Predictably You'll Stay

Dopamine is often thought of as the "reward" chemical. But that's a bit of a misconception: the effects of dopamine aren't actually experienced when you successfully achieve a goal — they're activated when your brain thinks that something good might happen if you take action. And it's released in its highest concentrations when your brain associates high value with a potential reward, but isn't quite certain you'll actually get it.

In this way, dopamine relates more to how we anticipate the value of a reward than the reward itself, and its main job is to prime us with the motivation we require to act.

You can see this behavioral feedback loop illustrated very clearly in the way video games manufacture engagement. A well-designed game intentionally pushes right up against the edge of a player's skill level: by challenging them to a limit just below the threshold of difficulty that would make them prefer to quit, and letting them win in mostly predictable ways while inhabiting this narrow window, they produce just the right amount of value with the right amount of uncertainty that allows games to be experienced as continually rewarding — sometimes even to the point of clinical addiction. In fact, both ICD-11 and DSM-5 now formally recognize gaming-related and internet use disorders.

Social media platforms regularly use similar principles to promote sustained user engagement. One of the most direct ways they do this is through a mechanism called intermittent variable reinforcement — the exact same feature that makes slot machines so hard to walk away from. In both cases, the set up is identical: you're repeatedly pairing the same output (pulling a lever, swiping a finger) with just enough reward to keep you coming back, never so much that you leave feeling ultimately satisfied.

Remember that, when you're scrolling a feed, you can't know ahead of time exactly what the next post will be. You hope it'll be something meaningful, relatable, or interesting. But the reality is that it could just as easily be something boring, or something that makes you feel anxious or angry. The underlying reality is that it will often strategically include all of these: sometimes the emotional whiplash will be obvious, other times it will be subtle to the point of being borderline undetectable. But rest assured, it's still very much personally tailored.

Another way that your dopamine is used to keep you chronically online is through A/B Testing — the kind of behavioral experimentation you probably had no idea you'd be a part of when you signed up for social media, but likely have been subject to more than a few times by now. It involves creating small variations of platform or content features, then testing them on different user groups to isolate what specific variables are driving the most engagement. These variations can include anything from the color of a notification, to video length, to the exact wording used in titles or captions.

Meta (Facebook & Instagram) Uses Meta Ads Manager to split-test ad creatives, audiences, and placements to optimize ad spend — meaning what you see is what performed best at keeping people like you engaged.
YouTube Utilizes the YouTube A/B Test tool directly in YouTube Studio to test up to three different titles and thumbnails for the highest watch time. The thumbnail you see wasn't chosen for clarity — it was chosen because it made people click.

More and more people are beginning to recognize and call out the addictive behavioral loops these tactics create. But the data that would allow independent researchers to fully study these effects is owned entirely by the platforms, and is currently protected as proprietary information and trade secrets. Accordingly, we're only beginning to understand the scale of this topic as an issue of public health.

Cortisol · The Stress Hormone
⚡

Why Ragebait and Fearmongering Always Work

Cortisol is your body's primary stress hormone, produced by your adrenal glands. When paired with adrenaline, a spike of cortisol during moments of intense stress triggers what we call a "fight or flight" response. This raises your heart rate, releases a quick burst of sugar from your liver for energy, diverts blood flow to your arms and legs to improve short-term strength and speed, and can temporarily boost your immune system.

But cortisol isn't only for moments of extreme stress: our bodies produce it regularly, and that's actually a good thing. We need healthy cortisol levels in order to properly regulate our blood pressure, metabolism, and sleep/wake cycles. That said, having too much or too little cortisol over a long period of time is bad for your health. A chronic imbalance can negatively affect appetite, weight, blood sugar, blood pressure, inflammation, immune function, and even muscle and bone health.

When we understand that cortisol is both a powerful part of our survival response and a necessary part of our body's everyday functioning, it becomes clear how important it is that we take care to keep it in balance.

And yet, platforms know there are few more powerful hooks than triggering your body's built-in threat detection system. The way we process negative content appears to drive significantly more engagement than how we tend to interact with positive content, so algorithms trained to optimize for engagement end up promoting media that capitalizes on our anxieties and vulnerability to outrage.

Exposure to negative content has been shown to cause momentary spikes in cortisol, and may also contribute to chronically elevated cortisol levels over time — which can manifest as exhaustion, anxiety, racing thoughts, irritability, and brain fog.

Melatonin + Cognitive Arousal · Sleep Disruption
🌙

A Perfect Recipe for a Disrupted Night's Sleep

You've probably heard that blue light suppresses melatonin and delays sleep. Maybe you've tried to compensate for this with dark mode, warm-tone filters, or reduced brightness at night. You might have even gotten a pair of blue light filter glasses at one point — which have unfortunately been shown to provide minimal to no benefit for reducing digital eye strain or meaningfully improving sleep.

But there's another, more significant contributor to sleep issues that's generally talked about less often, probably because it's harder to avoid: cognitive arousal. It's the exact reason why we hear we should "avoid screens for at least one hour before going to bed."

In previous sections, we outlined some of the ways that your biology is systematically activated by using tech. Putting the phone down doesn't mean you can simply switch those systems off. Your nervous system needs time to wind down from the stimulation it's been exposed to, and that process takes longer than most people realize. Good sleep hygiene might be "foundational" to our wellbeing, but it's not straightforward: we need to have the information to know what promotes and what inhibits healthy sleep patterns, and the skills are resources to actually act on them. This all further obscured by a culture that inherently deprioritizes the value of rest, and grossly over-emphasizes how much attention we should be putting into screetime.

The (Fragmented) Attention Economy
🧠

Your Focus is a Sacrifice They're Willing to Make

If you're a regular smartphone user and you've been feeling like you can't seem to focus the way you used to, the factors described above are likely playing a role.

A note: a lack of focus can also be a symptom of a variety of medical, psychiatric, and developmental conditions. If it's seriously affecting your life, it's worth talking to a qualified medical professional to rule out any underlying health issues.

The problem is, our ability to pay attention is a necessary part of our ability to read through things carefully, and to understand what we're saying "yes" to when we check the notorious "I agree" box every time we create a new account or download a new app.

When our capacity for sustained attention has become systematically eroded through prolonged exposure to ultra-processed, short-form content optimized for maximum stimulation per second, it's not only our brains that are being manipulated into structural change: our close social relationships, communities, and larger culture are all being reshaped alongside us.

The question is, is it really on our terms?
Why All of This Matters
🎯

Under the Influence of Screentime

I would like to take a brief moment to make something very clear: none of this is your fault. It didn't happen because you have a flawed personality of some sort, and it certainly isn't some kind of moral failure.

However, all of that has to be followed up with a very big "but":

Your willingness to take personal responsibility for this situation is going to be the main thing that helps you improve it.

The Reveal

Every element of that feed
was designed.

Not by accident. Not as a side effect. By people who understood exactly what it would do to your brain, and optimized for it.

  • ⚡Ragebait triggered your cortisol. Posts designed to provoke anger or fear activate your threat detection system. Your heart rate rose slightly. Your attention narrowed. You were drawn in to attend to a perceived threat — even knowing it was a simulation.
  • 🎰The like count is a slot machine. When you interacted, your brain anticipated a reward. When you scrolled past without engaging, it wondered whether the next post might be more worth it. That wondering is your dopamine loop running exactly as designed.
  • 📜Infinite scroll removed your stopping point. There was no natural end to that feed. Real feeds work the same way — the moment where you'd naturally pause and decide whether to continue has been deliberately engineered out of existence.
  • 🧠Your attention capacity degraded with every swipe. Each post demanded a rapid evaluation — is this worth my time? — then an immediate reset for the next one. Repeated enough times, this trains your brain to expect nothing to require sustained focus.
  • 🎯The content mix was deliberate. Cute content lowered your guard. Alarming content spiked your engagement. Alternating between the two kept you emotionally off-balance — scrolling to resolve a tension that the feed is specifically designed to never let resolve.
